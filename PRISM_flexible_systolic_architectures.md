# PRISM: A Polymorphic Systolic Fabric with Multi-Axis Runtime Reconfiguration

### A Synthesis, Critique, and Advancement of Flexible Systolic Array Architectures

*A research proposal building on ONE-SA, Flex-TPU, CogSys, NSFlow, FlexiSAGA, BitSys, and SNAKE*

---

## Abstract

Systolic arrays remain the dominant compute substrate for dense tensor workloads, but a growing body of work demonstrates that rigid, single-mode arrays leave substantial performance on the table when workloads are heterogeneous. Seven recent architectures each attack one axis of this rigidity: Flex-TPU reconfigures the dataflow per layer; SNAKE reshapes the logical array geometry per operator; BitSys recomposes arithmetic precision at runtime; FlexiSAGA exploits structured weight sparsity across seven dataflows; ONE-SA folds nonlinear activation functions into the array itself; and CogSys and NSFlow extend the array to heterogeneous neural-plus-symbolic operator mixes with co-designed scheduling. Each work shows that its single axis of flexibility costs only a modest steering overhead — typically five to fifteen percent of area — and returns utilization gains of 1.4× to 4× on the workloads that stress that axis. Yet no existing design recognizes that these five axes of flexibility (dataflow, shape, precision, sparsity, and function type) share most of their underlying hardware mechanisms and interact strongly in the configuration space: the optimal dataflow depends on the tile shape, which depends on the effective precision, which interacts with the sparsity pattern, and so on.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it presents a structured comparison of the seven architectures, extracting six transferable design principles and identifying the specific weakness each design leaves unaddressed. Second, it proposes PRISM (Polymorphic Reconfigurable Inference Systolic Machine), a unified fabric that supports all five flexibility axes through a shared mechanism set — a rigid-core/flexible-seam tile organization, a routing-program abstraction for per-tile configuration, precision-shape exchange, elastic sparse streaming, resident-tile epilogue fusion for nonlinear functions, and configuration-wavefront reconfiguration — at a target overhead below the sum of the individual axes' costs. Third, it lays out a complete, phased methodology for designing, implementing, verifying, and evaluating PRISM, including a parameterized RTL generator that can instantiate each prior architecture as a degenerate configuration, enabling the first genuinely iso-methodology comparison across this design space. We articulate falsifiable hypotheses, an ablation plan that measures the marginal utility and super-additivity of each flexibility axis, and a risk analysis with concrete mitigations.

## 1. Introduction

The systolic array, introduced by Kung and Leiserson in the late 1970s and revived at datacenter scale by Google's TPU, owes its efficiency to three properties: nearest-neighbor communication, regular data movement, and the near-total absence of per-element control. These same properties are the source of its fragility. A fixed N×N array with a fixed dataflow, fixed operand precision, fixed dense arithmetic, and support only for multiply-accumulate achieves peak efficiency on exactly one workload shape and degrades — sometimes catastrophically — on everything else.

Modern inference workloads are precisely the "everything else." A single large language model decode pass mixes wide, skinny GEMMs whose M dimension tracks the batch size, attention operators whose shapes track sequence length and head configuration, softmax and normalization epilogues, and increasingly quantized or sparsified weight matrices; mixture-of-experts models add routing-dependent dynamic operator shapes; neurosymbolic systems interleave GEMMs with vector-symbolic circular convolutions; quantized edge networks vary their precision layer by layer. Between 2024 and 2026, at least seven architectures appeared that each relax one constraint of the classical array, and each reports that the relaxation is cheap relative to its benefit. The natural question — what does a fabric look like that relaxes them all, coherently — has not been asked, let alone answered. That is the question this paper takes up.

The first half of the paper (Sections 2 and 3) compares the seven source architectures along common dimensions: which flexibility axis they target, what hardware mechanism implements it, at what granularity reconfiguration happens, what it costs, and what it returns. From that comparison we distill six design principles and a list of the gaps each work leaves open. The second half (Sections 4 through 7) proposes PRISM, an architecture that unifies the five flexibility axes; develops a detailed methodology for building and evaluating it; and states the hypotheses and risks honestly, including the conditions under which the unified approach would fail to justify itself.

A note on intellectual honesty: an architecture that does everything risks doing nothing well. The central scientific claim of PRISM is not that more flexibility is always better — it is that the five axes share enough hardware that their union costs less than their sum, and that workload heterogeneity within single modern models is now large enough that per-operator multi-axis configuration beats any single-axis specialist on a geomean basis. Both claims are falsifiable, and the methodology in Section 6 is designed to falsify them if they are wrong.

## 2. The Seven Architectures: A Structured Survey

This section summarizes each work in its own terms before comparing them. Throughout, "PE" denotes a processing element, and the dataflow taxonomy follows the standard input-stationary (IS), weight-stationary (WS), and output-stationary (OS) classification.

### 2.1 ONE-SA: Nonlinear Functions Inside the Array

ONE-SA (Sun et al., arXiv:2402.00395) observes that ASIC accelerators pair a systolic array with dedicated nonlinear function units (for GELU, softmax, layer normalization, and the like), and that this pairing makes the accelerator model-specific: the type and quantity of nonlinear units must be tailored to each network, and imperfect pipelining between the matrix unit and the function units causes stalls. Its remedy is to approximate every nonlinear function with capped piecewise linearization (CPWL): the function is cut into segments, each approximated as y = kx + b, with the segment parameters precomputed and indexed by segment number. Execution decomposes into Intermediate Parameter Fetching (IPF) — computing each input element's segment index and gathering the corresponding slope/intercept matrices K and B — and a Matrix Hadamard Product (MHP), Y = X ⊙ K + B, executed on the array.

The microarchitectural support is twofold. The L3 buffer gains a data-addressing module (a shift-based segment calculator, a range-capping scaler, and parameter buffers) and a data-rearrangement module that interleaves k with b and x with the constant 1 so that the two-input array can consume three logical matrices. The PEs gain two control signals that partition them into "computation PEs" on the diagonal — the only ones that actually multiply during MHP, because Hadamard products have no data reuse — and "transmission PEs" everywhere else, which merely forward operands. On a Virtex-7 FPGA at INT16, the additions cost essentially no BRAM, LUT, or DSP and 13.3–24.1% more flip-flops; accuracy losses are negligible at fine approximation granularity (0.25 is the default); and the resulting single-unit accelerator reaches 83–136% of the efficiency of model-specific accelerators while running CNNs, BERT, and GCNs on one substrate.

The weakness is utilization: during MHP, an N×N array performs only N useful multiplies per cycle — O(N) of O(N²) — because only diagonal PEs compute. ONE-SA accepts this because nonlinear operations are a small fraction of total work, but the design leaves a factor of N on the table during every epilogue, and its parameter round-trip through DRAM (segment indices written out, parameters fetched back) adds memory traffic that a fused design could avoid.

### 2.2 Flex-TPU: Per-Layer Dataflow Reconfiguration

Flex-TPU (Elbtity, Chandarana, and Zand, arXiv:2407.08700) starts from a layer-wise profiling observation: across ResNet-18 and six other CNNs, the cycle-optimal dataflow differs layer by layer — early layers favor weight-stationary, middle layers output-stationary, late layers input-stationary — so any single static dataflow leaves performance behind. The fix is small: each PE gains one extra register and two multiplexers, allowing the stationary operand to be the input, the output (partial sum), or the weight, selected per layer by a Configuration Management Unit that also drives a Dataflow Generator producing the matching memory address sequences. The optimal per-layer dataflow is found by a one-time pre-deployment sweep (run the model three times, once per dataflow, keep the per-layer winner).

Synthesized in 45nm, the overhead is 10.1–13.6% area, 7.6–10.7% power, and at most 2.07% critical-path delay across 8×8 to 32×32 arrays. The reconfigurability returns average speedups of 1.612×, 1.090×, and 1.400× over static IS, OS, and WS respectively at 32×32, and the advantage grows with scale — 1.238× over the best static dataflow at 128×128 and 1.349× at 256×256.

The limitations are scope and selection method. Only the dataflow axis is flexible; shapes, precision, sparsity, and operator types are fixed. The exhaustive three-run selection does not scale to large configuration spaces, and the per-layer granularity cannot react to dynamic shapes (batch, sequence length) unknown before deployment.

### 2.3 CogSys: Reconfigurable Neural/Symbolic Execution

CogSys (Wan et al., HPCA 2025, arXiv:2503.01162) targets vector-symbolic neurosymbolic workloads (NVSA, MIMONet, LVRF, PrAE), whose profiles show symbolic operations — dominated by blockwise circular convolution — consuming up to 87% of runtime while contributing only 19% of FLOPs, because GPUs and TPU-like arrays execute them as memory-bound GEMVs with O(d²) materialized circulant matrices and no parallelism across convolutions. CogSys contributes on three levels. Algorithmically, an iterative factorization replaces the symbolic codebook (13.5 MB → 190 KB; 71× footprint reduction) by unbinding and projecting in superposition, accelerated by injected stochasticity and INT8/FP8 quantization. Architecturally, a reconfigurable neuro/symbolic PE (nsPE) with four registers (stationary, passing, streaming, partial-sum) supports three modes — load, GEMM, and circular convolution — where the new "bubble streaming" dataflow holds vector A stationary and streams vector B through passing registers that impose a one-cycle skew per PE, realizing the circular shift in-array with O(d) memory footprint and column-wise parallelism (each array column runs an independent convolution). Spatial and temporal mapping modes trade bandwidth against latency for k convolutions of dimension d, with end-to-end latency 3M + d − 1 cycles per convolution on an M-PE column. At the system level, an adaptive workload-aware scheduler (adSCH) interleaves the symbolic kernels of one task batch into the idle array regions of another batch's neural layers, with cell-wise and column-wise partitioning, planned offline by a greedy search over a bounded space.

The results are striking: under TSMC 28nm, reconfigurable support costs under 5% area over a TPU-like array (4.8% at FP8) with one-cycle mode switching, and yields up to 75.96× speedup over TPU-like execution of circular convolution, 4–96× over GPUs end-to-end, and the first real-time (under 0.3 s) abduction reasoning at 4 mm² and 1.48 W. An ablation attributes 28% runtime reduction to scheduling, rising to 61% and 71% with the scalable array and reconfigurable PE included. A design-choice study shows reconfigurable PEs beat heterogeneous (separate neural and symbolic) PE pools: same-area heterogeneous designs double latency at 45% utilization versus 90%.

The limitation is again single-axis scope: shapes, precision (beyond global quantization), and sparsity are out of scope, and the scheduling search, while bounded, is bespoke to the neurosymbolic operator mix.

### 2.4 NSFlow: End-to-End Generation of Flexible Dataflow Architectures

NSFlow (Yang, Wan et al., arXiv:2504.19323) shares CogSys's workload target but shifts the contribution to design automation for FPGAs. Its frontend extracts an execution trace from the PyTorch program, builds a dataflow graph that captures critical paths, inner-loop and inter-loop parallelism, runtime cost functions, and memory footprints per node, and then runs a two-phase design-space exploration: phase one fixes the hardware configuration (sub-array height H, width W, count N) under a static partition assumption; phase two fine-tunes the per-node partition between neural and vector-symbolic sub-arrays. This collapses a 10³⁰⁰ design space to roughly 10³ evaluations. The backend instantiates a parameterized template: an adaptive systolic array (AdArray) whose column-pair sub-arrays either fuse for GEMM or operate singly for circular convolution (with the same passing-register streaming trick as CogSys), a re-organizable memory system whose MemA splits or merges at runtime to feed parallel neural/symbolic execution, mixed INT8/INT4 precision with DSP-efficient multipliers and LUT adders, and a custom SIMD unit. Deployed on an AMD U250 at 272 MHz, NSFlow reports 31× speedup over Jetson TX2, more than 2× over an RTX 2080, up to 8× over a 128×128 TPU-like array, and more than 3× over Xilinx DPU, with mixed precision achieving 5.8× weight-memory savings at sub-1% accuracy loss. An ablation shows speedup against a static array grows with the symbolic fraction of the workload (over 7× at 80% symbolic memory share) and that phase-two mapping refinement contributes up to 44% when neural and symbolic work are balanced.

NSFlow's distinctive contribution is the compiler: it is the only one of the seven that derives the architecture from the workload automatically. Its weaknesses are inherited from its template (two flexibility axes — operator type and a folding-based shape adjustment — plus design-time precision) and from FPGA-specific assumptions that do not directly transfer to ASIC timing closure.

### 2.5 FlexiSAGA: Sparse and Dense Processing Across Seven Dataflows

FlexiSAGA (Müller, Lübeck et al., arXiv:2506.01566) addresses weight sparsity. It supports three dense dataflows (dIS, dWS, dOS) and four sparse ones (sIS, sWS, sOS using a two-stage bitmap format, plus csOS using a new Compressed Sparse Block format that greedily merges complementary sparse columns so that several partial columns load as one), with a decompression unit (DecU) arbitrating memory access and emitting zeros for pruned weights so the array never sees them as work, and a controller that skips entire zero or merged columns. Crucially, the pruning algorithm is co-designed: structured-sparsity learning prunes row or column vectors whose length n equals the array dimension, iteratively raising sparsity while staying within a 2% accuracy budget, so the sparsity pattern aligns exactly with what the hardware can skip. Evaluated as a cycle-approximate virtual prototype (Amaranth HDL) across AlexNet, VGG16, GoogLeNet, and ResNet50, FlexiSAGA achieves whole-network sparse-over-dense speedups of 1.41–4.28×, beating DeepSparse on Xeon and ARM CPUs, TensorRT 2:4 sparsity on an Orin GPU, and the one-sided SCNN and SparTen accelerators on mean operator-wise speedup. Two findings stand out for our purposes: per-operator dataflow selection matters enormously (the csOS dataflow wins 40.2% of operators, but every dataflow wins somewhere), and a design-space exploration over array shape × dataflow × pruning vector orientation shows the global optimum is non-obvious (a 4×18 array with column pruning n=4 won their whole-network study) — motivating exactly the kind of joint search PRISM proposes, but which FlexiSAGA performs only at design time and over fewer axes.

The limitations: input/activation sparsity is unexploited (weight-side only, fixed at deployment), there is no precision flexibility, the evaluation platform is a unit-latency memory model rather than synthesized hardware, and the 1440-point DSE took 421 hours of simulation — underscoring the need for analytical models.

### 2.6 BitSys: Bit-Level Precision and Channel Recomposition

BitSys (Liu, Ullah, Kumar, arXiv:2602.23334) targets mixed-precision quantized networks, motivated by the observation that fixed 8-bit multipliers waste the speedup potential of 1/2/4-bit layers — a Vivado-IP accelerator ran a 1/2/4/8-bit mixed MLP only 5% faster than a uniform 8-bit one because all operands were padded to 8 bits. BitSys decomposes an 8×8-bit multiplication into a triangular array of 1-bit partial products a_i·b_j, organized as a bitwise systolic array in which each bitwise PE (an AND, or XNOR for binarized networks, plus mask logic and valid bits) maps onto exactly one Xilinx LUT6_2 primitive. Runtime-selected sub-partial-product masks reconfigure the same fabric as eight 1-bit channels, four 2-bit channels, two 4-bit channels, or one 8-bit channel, signed or unsigned, with a fixed left-shift schedule shared across all modes (channel-offset shifts fold into the partial-product shifts so the output pipeline never changes). An output-generator pipeline with carry-cutters prevents cross-channel carry pollution, and a tree-structured accumulator input converter reduces multi-channel outputs into a single accumulator per multiplier. The result has the lowest total path delay (1.357–1.719 ns; 250–500 MHz on Ultra96-V2), the best area-delay and power-delay products among compared multi-precision multipliers, and a systolic-array accelerator that is 1.32–3.57× faster than prior designs on mixed-precision inference — with the systolic organization itself credited for simplifying data streaming relative to single-layer accelerators.

The limitations: deep pipelines (22–27 cycles of latency per multiplier), lower hardware utilization at low precision (masked sub-products idle, a trade-off the authors acknowledge as inherent to fixed-input-width bit-parallel designs), and no interaction with array-level concerns — shape, dataflow, and sparsity are untouched, and the precision gain is not converted into logical array geometry.

### 2.7 SNAKE: Shape and Dataflow Reconfiguration for Near-Memory LLM Decode

SNAKE (Ai et al., arXiv:2604.04253) is the most recent and most architecturally sophisticated of the seven. Its setting is 3D-stacked near-memory processing for LLM decode, where internal bandwidth (24 TB/s) is so high that decode operators flip from memory-bound to compute-bound, making the logic die's severely limited area (roughly 76 mm² active) the first-order constraint. Three insights follow. First, systolic arrays beat the MAC trees used in prior NMP designs by 8.23× in iso-function RTL area, because MAC trees pay for high-fanout operand delivery and multi-stage reduction networks. Second, decode GEMMs are "fat" (M ≪ N, K, with M tracking batch size), so a fixed near-square array idles most rows; SNAKE remaps a physical 64×64 array into elongated logical shapes (8×512, 16×256, 32×128) by snaking the dataflow serpentine-fashion through the fabric, with reconfiguration granularity 8 and one-cycle mode switching, and supports both OS and IS dataflows selected per operator by whether N or K dominates. Third, high local bandwidth weakens the case for large double-buffered SRAMs, so buffer area is reallocated to PEs (8×512 logical PEs at 240 KB beats 8×128 at 719 KB), and the vector core is fused with the array — sharing a banked 2R2W output buffer rather than private multi-ported SRAM — yielding 4.00× compute-area efficiency over the MAC-tree baseline. A multi-PU scheduling framework reduces inter-core parallelization to four modes (IS-S, IS-ST, OS-S, OS-ST over spatial/spatio-temporal N and K partitioning), selected per operator by a lightweight simulator search; fixed-mode scheduling loses 1.04–6.43× versus per-operator selection. End-to-end, SNAKE delivers 2.91× speedup and 2.40× energy efficiency over Stratum, and 2.33×/3.00× over fixed 48×48 and 8×288 arrays — direct evidence that reconfigurability itself, not just elongation, is the win.

The limitations: precision is fixed (FP16), sparsity is unexploited (notable, since MoE expert weights are increasingly quantized and pruned), nonlinear operators ride on a conventional vector unit rather than the array, the 800 MHz frequency (versus 1 GHz for fixed shapes) shows the timing tax of fine-grained boundary flexibility, and the minimum reshaping granularity of 8 strands GEMV-like M=1 operators (excused as memory-bound, which is true in the NMP setting but not in general).

## 3. Synthesis: What the Field Has Learned, and What It Is Missing

### 3.1 Six Transferable Design Principles

Reading the seven works side by side, six principles recur often enough, and independently enough, to be treated as established design knowledge rather than isolated results.

**Principle 1 — Flexibility lives in the steering layer, not the compute layer.** Every one of the seven designs achieves its flexibility by adding registers and multiplexers around an unchanged multiply-accumulate core: Flex-TPU adds one register and two muxes; CogSys adds a passing register and per-PE crossbars; SNAKE adds boundary crossbars and register paths; BitSys's entire reconfiguration is mask and shift selection around fixed AND gates; FlexiSAGA's sparsity machinery sits in the controller and decompression unit, not the PEs. The recurring cost figure for one axis of steering is five to fifteen percent of array area with negligible (under ~2%) critical-path impact when the muxing is local. The corollary cuts both ways: naively stacking five independent axes of per-PE steering could plausibly cost 40–60% of area and badly damage timing — which is exactly why a unified design must share mechanisms rather than concatenate them.

**Principle 2 — One physical mesh, many logical machines.** CogSys's bubble streaming and SNAKE's serpentine remapping are structurally the same move: repurpose nearest-neighbor links, plus one extra register and a routing choice, to make the fixed 2D mesh emulate a different machine — a bank of independent convolution pipelines in one case, an elongated array in the other. ONE-SA's transmission-PE mode is a third instance (the mesh emulating a routing network), and NSFlow's column-pair folding a fourth. None of the papers frames this as a general capability, but together they demonstrate that the physical mesh is best understood as a substrate over which a small *routing program* defines the logical machine. PRISM elevates this to the central abstraction.

**Principle 3 — Selection must be per-operator, and switching must be nearly free.** Flex-TPU's per-layer wins, FlexiSAGA's finding that every one of its seven dataflows is optimal for some operator, SNAKE's 1.04–6.43× penalty for fixed scheduling, and CogSys's interleaving gains all point the same way: the unit of configuration is the operator (or tile), not the model, and the architectures that win make mode switching cost one cycle (CogSys, SNAKE) or hide it in pipeline fill (BitSys's three-cycle layer reconfiguration). Any design whose reconfiguration cost is visible at operator granularity forfeits the benefit.

**Principle 4 — The memory and buffer system is half the reconfiguration story.** ONE-SA's most intricate engineering is in its L3 buffer (segment addressing, parameter interleaving); NSFlow's memory blocks split and merge at runtime; FlexiSAGA's DecU makes sparsity invisible to the array; SNAKE's boundary SRAMs carry the multi-port burden of reshaping and its largest single win comes from reallocating buffer area to compute under high local bandwidth. A flexible array fed by a rigid memory system is not flexible.

**Principle 5 — Hardware/algorithm co-design multiplies the hardware's return.** FlexiSAGA's pruning is shaped to the array's vector length; BitSys assumes Brevitas-style quantization-aware training; ONE-SA's accuracy is controlled by an approximation granularity chosen per task difficulty; CogSys redesigns the factorization algorithm itself. In every case the algorithmic concession is small (≤2% accuracy) and the hardware return is large (1.4–4×). The compiler and training loop must know the array.

**Principle 6 — Scheduling spaces explode, and phased analytical search tames them.** NSFlow collapses 10³⁰⁰ configurations to ~10³ via a two-phase decomposition with closed-form runtime models; SNAKE collapses multi-core scheduling to four named modes; CogSys bounds its greedy search below O(10³) per step; FlexiSAGA, which lacks analytical models, paid 421 hours of simulation for 1,440 design points. The lesson: invest in validated closed-form cycle models first, search second.

### 3.2 The Gaps

Against those principles, each work leaves specific value unclaimed, and the set of gaps is itself structured.

**Gap A — Single-axis tunnel vision.** Each architecture is flexible along exactly one primary axis and rigid along the other four. Yet the axes interact: SNAKE's optimal logical shape depends on M, which at INT4 could be served by half the physical rows BitSys-style lane splitting would free; FlexiSAGA's optimal dataflow depends on the sparsity format, which changes the effective K dimension, which changes SNAKE-style shape selection; ONE-SA's epilogue cost depends on whether outputs are resident in the array (OS) or already drained (WS/IS) — a dataflow decision. No existing scheduler can even express these couplings because no existing hardware exposes them jointly.

**Gap B — Wasted fabric during non-GEMM modes.** ONE-SA uses N of N² PEs for Hadamard work. CogSys's circular-convolution mode uses each column independently but cannot fuse the SIMD epilogue into the array. SNAKE's vector core is fused at the buffer level but still a separate engine. Principle 2 says the mesh can emulate other machines; none of the works emulates a *full-fabric* element-wise machine, even though the serpentine mechanism that would enable it already exists in SNAKE.

**Gap C — Reconfiguration is sequenced, not pipelined.** All seven works switch configuration between operators: drain, reconfigure (1–3 cycles), refill. None overlaps the configuration of the next operator with the drain wave of the current one, even though systolic drain proceeds as a predictable spatial wavefront — an open invitation for shadowed configuration loading.

**Gap D — Evaluation incommensurability.** The seven results span Virtex-7, Ultra96, U250, 45nm Nangate, TSMC 28nm, ASAP7, a unit-latency virtual prototype, and three different simulators; baselines are reimplementations of each other at varying fidelity (SNAKE reimplements a MAC tree; FlexiSAGA compares against numbers reported in a third paper). The field cannot currently answer "which flexibility axis buys the most per unit area?" because no two axes have ever been measured with the same methodology. This is a gap a unified, feature-flagged RTL generator can close as a side effect of building the unified architecture.

**Gap E — Static selection in a dynamic world.** Flex-TPU and FlexiSAGA select configurations before deployment; NSFlow at compile time; CogSys and SNAKE offline per known operator graphs. But LLM serving has dynamic batch sizes, sequence-length distributions, and MoE expert loads that shift minute by minute. SNAKE's own data shows the preferred logical shape tracks batch size; nobody closes the loop at runtime.

## 4. PRISM: A Polymorphic Reconfigurable Inference Systolic Machine

### 4.1 Design Philosophy and Overview

PRISM is organized around a single thesis: the five flexibility axes — dataflow, logical shape, precision, sparsity, and function mode — can share one mechanism set, so their union costs far less than their sum. Concretely, the shared mechanisms are (i) a small per-PE register file with configurable operand steering, which simultaneously provides Flex-TPU's stationary-operand selection, CogSys's bubble register, and the chain links needed for serpentine and vector modes; (ii) a hierarchical *rigid-core / flexible-seam* organization that confines expensive routing flexibility to tile boundaries, protecting frequency; (iii) boundary buffer and stream machinery (decompression, parameter fetch, address generation) that serves sparsity, nonlinear epilogues, and reshaping from one port budget; and (iv) a single configuration abstraction — the routing program — interpreted identically by every tile, on top of which a two-level scheduler (offline compiler plus runtime persona selector) chooses per-operator configurations.

The fabric is a P×P grid of rigid T×T compute tiles (T = 8 by default, matching SNAKE's empirically sufficient granularity), joined by programmable seams, ringed by four boundary buffer complexes, and governed by a controller that maintains a region table of active logical machines. A default mid-range instance is P = 8, T = 8 (64×64 PEs, 4,096 MACs at 8-bit native), but every dimension is a generator parameter; Section 6 treats instance sizing as an output of the methodology, not an input.

### 4.2 The Rigid-Core / Flexible-Seam Organization

The central physical-design idea, and the answer to the overhead-stacking problem of Principle 1, is hierarchy. Inside a tile, the interconnect is a classical hard-macro systolic mesh: operands flow west-to-east, partial sums north-to-south, with only the cheap, local, timing-friendly per-PE additions described in Section 4.3. Tiles are placed-and-routed once as macros and instantiated P² times — exactly the regularity that lets fixed arrays close timing at high frequency.

All expensive flexibility lives at the seams: registered switchboxes between tile edges that can (a) pass through, making adjacent tiles act as one larger array; (b) turn, connecting the east edge of one tile row to the east edge of the next row — the boustrophedon connection that realizes SNAKE-style serpentine shapes at tile granularity; (c) cut, isolating tile groups so different regions of the fabric run different modes concurrently (generalizing NSFlow's AdArray folding and CogSys's cell-wise partitioning); and (d) inject/extract, connecting a seam to a boundary-buffer lane so interior tile groups have their own logical edges. Because seams are registered, crossing one costs a deterministic cycle of skew, which the address generators and the compiler's cycle model absorb — the same discipline SNAKE applies to its boundary, extended inward.

The granularity argument is quantitative. Per-PE shape flexibility (the SNAKE approach pushed to granularity 1) requires multi-port boundary access and per-PE crossbar paths whose cost SNAKE measured at a 20% frequency penalty even at granularity 8. Tile-granularity flexibility needs only P−1 seam columns and rows of switchboxes — for P = 8, T = 8, that is 49 switchboxes serving 4,096 PEs, an overhead we budget at under 5% of array area — while still expressing every logical shape with both dimensions a multiple of T. The minimum logical shape granularity of T = 8 inherits SNAKE's justification (operators with M < 8 are bandwidth-bound in every memory system we target, so the stranded rows cost little), and Section 4.5 shows precision-shape exchange further softens this floor.

### 4.3 The Polymorphic PE and the Routing-Program Abstraction

Each PE contains one composable MAC (Section 4.5), four operand registers (stationary, streaming, passing, partial-sum — the CogSys complement, which we adopt because it is the superset needed by all modes), a valid bit (from BitSys, which doubles as a clock-gating enable and a sparse-stream qualifier), and source-select multiplexers on the multiplier and adder inputs. The configuration word per tile — not per PE; all PEs in a tile share it, except the boundary row/column which carry two extra bits — encodes a routing program with five fields: which register is pinned (stationary operand: input, weight, or output → IS/WS/OS, per Flex-TPU); whether the passing register is engaged (one-cycle skew → bubble streaming for circular convolution and correlation, per CogSys); whether the partial-sum chain is intact, cut (independent per-PE accumulation), or reversed at the seam (serpentine continuation); whether the multiplier consumes (stream × pinned) or (stream × stream) pairs (Hadamard/vector mode, Section 4.6); and lane configuration for precision (Section 4.5).

This is deliberately *not* a CGRA: there is no per-PE instruction stream, no dynamic routing, no flow control inside a tile. Timing remains statically scheduled and systolic. The routing program merely selects which of a small, enumerated family of systolic machines the tile group implements this operator. The enumeration is the union of the seven papers' modes plus the new ones below — about a dozen distinct logical machines, encodable in a 16-bit tile configuration word — and the claim that this stays cheap is testable: the per-PE additions are exactly Flex-TPU's (≈10–13%) plus CogSys's (<5%) plus the lane-split overhead of Section 4.5 (~10–15% over a fixed 8-bit MAC, per BitFusion-class precedent), which overlap in their multiplexer structure. Our area budget for the polymorphic PE is +25% over a rigid 8-bit OS PE, with seam and boundary machinery bringing the fabric total to a target of +30% — against which the evaluation must show ≥1.8× geomean utilization gains to claim a perf-per-area win (Section 6.7).

### 4.4 Unified Mode Family

With the mechanisms above, one fabric realizes the following logical machines, each selected per operator and per tile group.

**Dense GEMM, three dataflows, any tile-granular shape.** The base mode subsumes Flex-TPU (dataflow choice) and SNAKE (shape choice) simultaneously, which neither offers: a fat decode GEMM can run as an 8×512 logical OS array when K dominates, an 8×512 IS array when N dominates, or — new — a 16×256 WS array when an unusually large M with heavy weight reuse favors it. Dataflow and shape are selected jointly by the compiler's cycle model, because they interact: elongation changes the stream lengths over which each dataflow amortizes its fill/drain costs.

**Circular convolution banks.** Engaging the passing register turns every tile column into an independent circular-convolution pipeline (CogSys's machine), but PRISM adds two compositions CogSys lacks: serpentine chaining concatenates tile columns into pipelines longer than the physical edge (supporting vector dimensions d up to T·P² without temporal folding), and the cut mode lets a subset of tile groups run convolution banks while the remainder runs GEMM — CogSys's interleaved scheduling realized spatially rather than temporally, eliminating its mode-switch serialization.

**Full-fabric vector mode (repairing ONE-SA).** Cutting the partial-sum chain and selecting stream×stream multiplication turns every PE into an independent a·k + b unit: the fabric becomes a P²T²-lane element-wise engine for Hadamard products, residual adds, and CPWL evaluation. Boundary bandwidth, not PE count, then bounds throughput — which is precisely why the next mode matters.

**Resident-tile CPWL epilogue (the fusion mode).** This is PRISM's most consequential new mode. After an output-stationary GEMM, the output tile is *already resident* in the partial-sum registers. Instead of draining it, applying activation elsewhere, and reloading (ONE-SA's round-trip through DRAM; SNAKE's trip through the vector core), PRISM applies CPWL in place: (1) each PE extracts its output's segment index — for power-of-two segment widths this is a bit-field selection with saturation, near-free in logic, reusing ONE-SA's shift-and-cap insight but in-PE; (2) indices drain southward through the existing partial-sum chain (T·P cycles for a column), and the boundary epilogue unit — ONE-SA's L3 addressing module relocated to PRISM's boundary complex — gathers the (k, b) parameter pairs; (3) parameters stream back northward while PEs apply y = k·x + b to the resident x. Total cost is roughly three column-traversals, ≈3·T·P cycles, to activate T²P² elements — versus T·P cycles merely to *drain* them once. The epilogue thus costs about three drain-times and saves the drain, the off-fabric function-unit pass, and in fused producer-consumer chains the reload of the next operator's input. Softmax and normalization, which need reductions, use a two-pass variant: a max/sum reduction rides the partial-sum chain (the adder tree the mesh already is), the boundary unit computes the scalar correction, and the CPWL pass applies exp or the normalization affine. The accuracy budget is governed by CPWL granularity exactly as ONE-SA characterizes (granularity 0.25 default; per-task tuning), and the parameter tables live in the boundary complex sized by the same analysis.

**Sparse streaming.** Weight tiles arrive in two-stage-bitmap or CSB format (FlexiSAGA), decompressed at the boundary by a DecU that the controller's region table coordinates: wholly-zero or merged columns shorten the injected stream for the affected logical columns, and per-element valid bits gate MACs for residual intra-column zeros (saving energy where cycles cannot be saved). Because skipping happens before injection, sparsity composes orthogonally with every dataflow and shape — recovering FlexiSAGA's seven-dataflow generality without seven bespoke control paths. The CSB column-merge search runs at deployment time in the compiler, with merge length n tied to the *logical*, not physical, column length — so the co-designed pruning of Principle 5 prunes against the shapes the scheduler intends to use.

**Precision modes.** Described next, since they deserve their own treatment.

### 4.5 Precision-Shape Exchange

BitSys converts low precision into more channels per multiplier; SNAKE converts workload shape into logical array shape. PRISM composes the two into a single scheduler-visible currency: *logical PE count and geometry*. Each PE's 8-bit MAC is built from four 4-bit sub-multipliers in the BitFusion/BitSys style (two composition levels only — 8-bit native, 4-bit dual-lane, optional 2-bit quad-lane — bounding mask and shift overhead, which BitSys shows grows with depth). When an operator's weights are INT4, each physical PE column presents as two logical columns; a physical 64×64 fabric becomes a logical 64×128 at 4-bit, and *combined with serpentine remapping*, an 8×1024 or 16×512 logical machine. Signed/unsigned selection and carry isolation follow BitSys's mask-and-cut scheme; the output-side channel offsets fold into the existing shift schedule so the accumulation pipeline is precision-invariant.

The payoff is that precision now relaxes the shape floor that limits SNAKE: a batch-8 decode operator with INT4 weights that would strand half of a granularity-8 fabric instead doubles its logical width, and the scheduler's configuration vector treats (precision, shape, dataflow) as one joint choice with one cycle model. The accuracy side of the bargain is enforced exactly as in BitSys and NSFlow: per-layer precision is assigned by quantization-aware training (Brevitas-class flows) under an accuracy budget, and the compiler refuses configurations the model card does not certify.

### 4.6 Configuration Wavefront: Shadowed Reconfiguration

Every tile's configuration word is double-buffered. Because systolic drain proceeds as a deterministic spatial wavefront (in OS, the output skews out row by row; in WS/IS, the stationary operand of the next tile loads behind the last streaming element of the current one), the controller broadcasts the *next* operator's routing program while the *current* operator drains, and each tile commits the shadow word the cycle its last live datum departs. The visible reconfiguration cost becomes zero for same-shape transitions and at most one seam-skew for shape changes — versus the drain-reconfigure-refill serialization implicit in all seven prior designs. The mechanism costs one extra 16-bit register and a commit comparator per tile (49–64 tiles, negligible), and it is what makes Principle 3's "per-operator selection" genuinely free even when operators are tiny, as MoE expert slices and attention heads are.

### 4.7 Boundary Complex and Memory System

Each fabric edge carries a boundary complex of banked, double-buffered SRAM with three attached engines: the DecU (sparse decode), the epilogue parameter unit (CPWL tables, segment gather, reduction scalars), and the address/stream generator (per-dataflow patterns, serpentine lane mapping, circular-shift offsets). Following SNAKE, multi-port SRAM is provisioned only on the weight-injection sides and only to the degree the maximum logical slice count requires (P ports split symmetrically across the left and right edges); following SNAKE's buffer-reallocation result, total SRAM capacity is a generator parameter swept per memory system — small under 3D-NMP bandwidth, larger under LPDDR — rather than fixed by convention. A modest vector/SIMD residue unit (CogSys/NSFlow-style) remains for the few operations that resist both the fabric's vector mode and the epilogue unit (e.g., top-k routing for MoE), sharing the output buffer per SNAKE's 2R2W fusion rather than owning private multi-ported storage.

### 4.8 Two-Level Scheduling: Offline Compiler, Runtime Personas

**Offline.** The compiler ingests a framework-level trace (NSFlow's extraction approach), builds the dependency graph with per-node cost functions, and assigns each operator a configuration vector c = (mode, dataflow, logical shape, precision, sparse format, fusion flags, tile-group partition). The search is phased per Principle 6: phase one chooses the fabric partition classes and the small set of logical shapes the model will use (bounding the persona table); phase two refines per-operator choices with an overlap-aware list scheduler that — generalizing CogSys's adSCH — co-schedules independent operator streams onto cut tile groups, and exploits the wavefront mechanism to chain producer-consumer pairs (GEMM → resident epilogue → next GEMM) without fabric idle. All cost functions are the closed-form cycle models of Section 6.2, so the search needs no simulation in the loop.

**Runtime.** Dynamic dimensions — batch size, sequence-length bucket, MoE expert load — index a persona table of pre-validated configuration vectors compiled for each bucket. A hardware sequencer applies the selected persona's per-operator configurations through the wavefront mechanism; selection latency is a table lookup, and every persona was certified offline for accuracy (precision/sparsity) and deadlock-freedom (stream lengths). This closes Gap E with strictly bounded runtime machinery: PRISM never searches online, it only *selects* online — a deliberate conservatism that keeps the runtime verifiable.

### 4.9 What Is Genuinely New Here

For clarity against the prior art: the rigid-core/flexible-seam hierarchy (4.2) is new as an explicit answer to flexibility's timing tax, taking SNAKE's granularity-8 result to its architectural conclusion; the routing-program unification of all seven papers' modes under one tile-level abstraction (4.3–4.4) is new; the resident-tile CPWL epilogue with in-PE segment extraction and parameter stream-back (4.4) is new and repairs both ONE-SA's O(N) utilization and its DRAM round-trip; precision-shape exchange (4.5) is a new composition that neither BitSys nor SNAKE can express alone; the configuration wavefront (4.6) is new; spatial (rather than temporal) co-residence of GEMM and convolution-bank modes via seam cuts (4.4) is new relative to CogSys; and the persona-table runtime (4.8) is the first bounded-online answer to dynamic shapes in this literature. Everything else — bubble streaming, serpentine traversal, CPWL mathematics, bitmap/CSB sparsity, stationary-operand muxing, two-phase DSE, buffer reallocation, systolic-vector buffer fusion — is deliberately inherited, with attribution, because it works.

## 5. Analytical Foundations: Cost Models for the Joint Configuration Space

Before any RTL exists, PRISM needs closed-form cycle and energy models for every mode, because the scheduler (Section 4.8) and the design-space exploration (Section 6.3) both depend on them. The models below extend the validated forms in the source papers to PRISM's joint space; deriving, implementing, and *validating* them against RTL is itself a methodology phase (Section 6.2). We sketch the structure here so the methodology is concrete.

For a dense GEMM of dimensions (M, N, K) on a logical array of H rows and W columns built from G tile groups with seam skew s per crossing, the per-tile latency generalizes NSFlow's form: t ≈ (2H′ + W′ + d₁ − 2 + σ) · ⌈d₂/H⌉ · ⌈d₃/W⌉, where (d₁, d₂, d₃) are the dataflow-dependent assignments of (M, N, K) to stream, row, and column dimensions, H′ and W′ are the logical dimensions including serpentine extension, and σ aggregates seam skews along the longest logical path (σ ≤ (P−1)·s, with s = 1 for registered seams). For circular convolution of k vectors of dimension d on column pipelines of length L, latency follows CogSys: t ≈ 3L + d − 1 per pipeline, parallelized across the column count, with serpentine chaining permitting L up to T·P² before temporal folding. The resident epilogue costs t ≈ 3·H′ + c_lut, where c_lut covers the boundary gather. Sparse execution scales the dense stream length by the expected surviving-column fraction: under CSB with merge length n and element sparsity ρ, the column-survival and merge statistics follow the Bernoulli analysis FlexiSAGA gives (whole-zero column probability ρⁿ, plus a merge-success term estimated empirically from the pruned model's actual masks, since greedy merging defeats closed-form treatment). Precision modes scale W (and the boundary stream width) by the lane count, with a fixed pipeline-depth adder per BitSys's composition depth. Energy models are factored per access type (MAC at each precision, register hop, seam hop, SRAM access by port count, DRAM access) with coefficients drawn initially from the source papers' technology-normalized numbers and replaced by post-synthesis extraction in Phase 3.

Two modeling subtleties deserve flagging now because they are where naive models fail. First, stall cycles: SNAKE shows the binding constraint flips between array cycles and memory-supply cycles as buffer capacity shrinks; the model must compute both and take the max per tile, with double-buffering feasibility as an explicit predicate. Second, overlap accounting: when the scheduler co-resides modes spatially (Section 4.4) or chains operators through the wavefront, latencies compose by critical path over the dependency graph, not by summation — the model library therefore exposes per-node (latency, occupancy, bandwidth) tuples that the scheduler's list scheduler consumes, rather than scalar costs.

## 6. Methodology: Designing, Implementing, and Testing PRISM

This section is a complete execution plan, organized as seven phases with explicit deliverables, tools, validation gates, and the hypotheses each phase tests. Indicative durations assume a small team (3–5 researchers) and sum to roughly twelve months with overlap; the phases are pipelined, not strictly sequential.

### 6.1 Phase 0 — Workload Corpus and Demand Characterization (weeks 1–6)

The first deliverable is a quantified statement of the problem PRISM claims to solve: how much heterogeneity, along which axes, exists inside modern inference workloads? We assemble an operator-level corpus spanning the seven papers' domains so no baseline can claim home-field disadvantage: CNNs (ResNet-50, MobileNet, GoogLeNet, VGG16, YOLO-Tiny — the Flex-TPU/FlexiSAGA set), transformers (BERT-base, ViT, LLaMA-3 8B and 70B decode at batch 1–64 and sequence buckets 1K–16K, Mixtral 8×22B and Qwen3-30B-A3B MoE — the SNAKE set), neurosymbolic workloads (NVSA, MIMONet, LVRF, PrAE on RAVEN/I-RAVEN/PGM — the CogSys/NSFlow set), and quantized/pruned variants produced with Brevitas (per BitSys: per-layer 1/2/4/8-bit assignments under a 2% accuracy budget) and structured pruning (per FlexiSAGA: vector lengths matched to candidate tile sizes, sparsity ramped under the same budget).

Operator traces are extracted with PyTorch FX (NSFlow's approach), and each operator is annotated with a five-axis demand vector: (M, N, K) shape and its dynamic-dimension dependencies; certified precision; achievable structured sparsity at each candidate vector length; nonlinear epilogue type and fusion opportunity; and operator-type class (GEMM, circular convolution, element-wise, reduction). Deliverables: the corpus, shape/precision/sparsity histograms per model family, a roofline placement of every operator class against three memory systems (LPDDR5 ~100 GB/s, HBM3 ~3 TB/s, 3D-NMP ~24 TB/s per SNAKE/Stratum), and a clustering of demand vectors that defines the representative configurations the hardware must serve well. Validation gate: the characterization must *quantitatively* reproduce the motivating observations of the source papers on their own workloads (e.g., per-layer dataflow optima flipping as in Flex-TPU Figure 1; symbolic runtime dominance as in CogSys Figure 4) — if it cannot, our tracing is wrong and nothing downstream is trustworthy.

### 6.2 Phase 1 — Analytical Models and Pre-RTL Design-Space Exploration (weeks 4–12, overlapping Phase 0)

Implement the model library of Section 5 as a Python package with a uniform interface: cost(operator, config, memory_system) → (cycles, stalls, energy, buffer demand). Unit-test every mode's model against hand-computed small cases and against the source papers' published numbers where reproducible (CogSys's 8-cycle versus 24-cycle three-convolution example; NSFlow's runtime equations; SNAKE's array-versus-stall crossover). Then run the first DSE: sweep tile size T ∈ {4, 8, 16}, fabric size P, boundary port count, SRAM capacity split, seam register depth, and precision-composition depth against the Phase-0 corpus under each memory system, optimizing geomean energy-delay product subject to an area budget parameterized from source-paper component costs. Deliverables: the chosen default instance(s) per deployment class (edge, HBM-attached, 3D-NMP), a sensitivity report identifying which flexibility axes matter for which workload family (the *hypothesis matrix* the final evaluation will test), and — critically — the pruned configuration menu (the dozen-odd logical machines of Section 4.4 with their parameter ranges) that bounds both the hardware enumeration and the verification space. Gate: model-predicted optima for single-axis sub-problems must agree with the source papers' findings (e.g., the model should independently rediscover that 16-MAC PEs sit on ONE-SA's Pareto front, that csOS dominates for high-sparsity layers, that elongated shapes win fat GEMMs) within stated error bars.

### 6.3 Phase 2 — RTL Generator and Functional Verification (weeks 8–28)

**Construction.** Implement a parameterized RTL generator (SystemVerilog or Chisel; Amaranth is a credible alternative given FlexiSAGA's experience) producing: the polymorphic PE; the rigid tile macro; the seam switchbox; the boundary complex (banked SRAM, DecU, epilogue parameter unit, stream generators); the controller (region table, persona sequencer, wavefront broadcast); and top-level fabric assembly. Every flexibility axis is a feature flag — `DATAFLOW_FLEX`, `SHAPE_FLEX`, `PRECISION_FLEX`, `SPARSE_FLEX`, `EPILOGUE_FLEX` — with the doubly important consequence that the generator can emit faithful analogues of each prior architecture (flags isolating one axis) and a rigid baseline (all flags off). This is how Gap D gets closed: every baseline in the evaluation is produced by the same codebase, synthesized by the same flow, and simulated by the same testbench.

**Verification strategy, bottom-up.** At the PE level: exhaustive or formally-bounded equivalence of the composable MAC against a golden arithmetic model across all precision/sign/lane configurations (SymbiYosys or a commercial equivalence tool; BitSys's carry-cut and sign-bit subtraction cases are the known-hazardous corners — its Figure 6 sign-location analysis becomes our checklist). At the tile level: directed tests per mode plus constrained-random operand streams checked against a NumPy golden model. At the fabric level: a constrained-random *operator* generator draws (mode, dataflow, shape, precision, sparsity mask, epilogue) tuples from the legal configuration menu, including adversarial mode-transition sequences to stress the wavefront mechanism; results are checked end-to-end against the golden model, with assertion-based monitors for stream alignment, valid-bit propagation, seam skew bookkeeping, and region-table consistency. Coverage model: all single modes, all mode-transition pairs, the cross of shape × dataflow × precision at tile granularity, sparse-stream length extremes (all-zero columns, no-merge worst cases), and epilogue saturation/capping boundaries. Deadlock-freedom of sparse variable-length streams is checked by model-checking a small (2×2-tile) instance with the stream protocol abstracted, then enforced structurally (streams are length-announced by the DecU before injection, so the fabric never speculates). Gate: 100% of the configuration menu covered, zero mismatches over a sustained randomized soak (we suggest 10⁹ randomized operator executions in fast functional simulation), and formal closure on the MAC.

### 6.4 Phase 3 — Physical Realization (weeks 20–36)

Two tracks, serving different evidentiary purposes.

**ASIC track (the headline numbers).** Synthesize and place-and-route the default instance and all feature-flag baselines in ASAP7 (open, and the node SNAKE used, easing cross-checks), with a 28nm commercial-node run if available (matching CogSys/Flex-TPU norms). SRAM macros via OpenRAM or FinCACTI-calibrated models, per SNAKE's practice. Extract per-configuration area breakdowns (the direct test of the +25% PE / +30% fabric budget of Section 4.3), achieved frequency (the direct test of the rigid-core/flexible-seam thesis — hypothesis H3 below — measured as the frequency gap between the full-flex instance and the rigid baseline, with SNAKE's 20% gap as the bar to beat), and post-layout power with switching activity from representative traces; feed extracted energy coefficients back into the Phase-1 models. If targeting the 3D-NMP deployment class, add a HotSpot thermal screen against the logic-die power budget per SNAKE/Stratum practice. Gate: timing closure of the full-flex instance within 15% of the rigid baseline's frequency; if missed, the mitigation ladder is (i) deepen seam pipelining, (ii) raise T to 16, (iii) demote the worst-offending axis to a tape-out-time option — and the result, even if negative, is publishable knowledge about where flexibility's timing tax actually lives.

**FPGA track (running-system truth).** Deploy a scaled instance (e.g., P = 4, T = 8 → 32×32 PEs) on an Alveo U250 or Versal board with an XRT host runtime, following NSFlow's and BitSys's integration patterns. Run real end-to-end inference: the BitSys mixed-precision MLP/CNN suite, a pruned CNN from the FlexiSAGA suite, a small transformer block with fused epilogues, and an NVSA reasoning slice. The FPGA track exists to catch what simulation cannot — host-interface effects, configuration-stream bring-up, persona-table operation under a live driver — and to provide unimpeachable functional demonstration; its absolute performance is explicitly *not* the headline metric, since FPGA mux costs distort the flexibility-overhead picture relative to ASIC.

### 6.5 Phase 4 — Cycle-Accurate System Simulation and Compiler (weeks 16–40)

Extend SCALE-Sim v3 with Ramulator memory backends (the SNAKE toolchain) to model every PRISM mode, multi-tile-group concurrency, the wavefront mechanism, and the boundary engines; calibrate against RTL simulation traces on a matrix of micro-kernels until cycle predictions are within ±5%, and lock the calibration with regression tests. In parallel, build the compiler of Section 4.8 (trace ingestion → graph → phased search → persona table → configuration stream) and a serving-level harness with Poisson request arrival, continuous batching, and P50/P90/P99 latency accounting, following SNAKE's Duplex-derived methodology so serving results are directly comparable. Gate: simulator-versus-RTL calibration holds across all modes, not just GEMM; compiler-emitted schedules are validated executable on the RTL testbench (the same configuration streams drive both).

### 6.6 Phase 5 — Evaluation Campaign (weeks 32–48)

**Baselines, all iso-methodology.** From the same generator and flow: the rigid OS array; single-axis instances corresponding to Flex-TPU (dataflow-only), SNAKE (shape+dataflow), BitSys-style (precision-only), FlexiSAGA-style (sparsity-only), and CogSys-style (operator-type-only); plus a MAC-tree compute substrate reimplemented per SNAKE's comparison; plus external reference points (GPU numbers via the source papers' published methodology where direct measurement is impractical). Iso-area normalization is mandatory and double-sided: baselines with lower per-PE overhead receive proportionally more PEs, so PRISM's flexibility must pay for the PEs it displaces.

**Benchmarks.** The full Phase-0 corpus, evaluated per memory system, in three regimes: static single-model inference (latency, energy, utilization per operator class); dynamic serving (LLM decode under varying request rates and batch/sequence distributions; MoE under expert-load skew — the regime that tests personas); and mixed-tenancy (concurrent neurosymbolic + CNN streams sharing the fabric via seam cuts — the regime that tests spatial co-residence).

**Ablations — the scientific core.** Leave-one-axis-out across the corpus, producing the marginal-utility table the field currently lacks: perf/area and energy contribution of each axis per workload family, measured under one methodology. A super-additivity test: does joint (precision + shape) selection beat the product of their individual gains on fat low-precision GEMMs, as Section 4.5 predicts? A scheduler ablation: fixed configuration versus per-operator offline versus persona-runtime, quantifying Gap E's cost (SNAKE's 1.04–6.43× fixed-mode penalty is the prior to beat or confirm). A fusion ablation: resident epilogue on versus off, measuring activation-related DRAM traffic and end-to-end latency. A reconfiguration ablation: wavefront on versus drain-then-switch, measured on operator-dense traces (MoE expert slices). Accuracy validation throughout: CPWL granularity sweeps reproducing ONE-SA's Table-III-style accuracy/granularity frontier on our corpus; quantization and pruning within their certified budgets, re-measured end-to-end on the actual hardware behavior (saturation, carry-cut) rather than emulated arithmetic.

### 6.7 Hypotheses and Success Criteria

The campaign tests five falsifiable hypotheses. **H1 (the thesis):** PRISM achieves ≥1.8× geomean performance-per-area over the *best* single-axis baseline across the heterogeneous corpus, while losing ≤10% to each specialist on that specialist's home workload. **H2 (mechanism sharing):** PRISM's measured area overhead over the rigid baseline is ≤30% and strictly less than the sum of the single-axis instances' overheads. **H3 (timing):** the rigid-core/flexible-seam organization holds the frequency penalty to ≤15% versus the rigid baseline (versus SNAKE's 20% at finer granularity). **H4 (wavefront):** visible reconfiguration overhead is <1% of cycles on operator-dense traces. **H5 (fusion):** resident epilogues eliminate ≥80% of activation-related off-fabric traffic versus drain-and-process, with end-to-end latency gains ≥10% on epilogue-heavy models. Each hypothesis has a pre-registered metric, and a *negative* result on any of them is reportable: if H2 fails, the field learns that flexibility axes do not share hardware as well as their mechanism descriptions suggest; if H1 fails on some workload family, the marginal-utility table says exactly which axis to drop for that deployment — which the feature-flagged generator turns directly into a product decision.

### 6.8 Risk Register and Mitigations

The principal risks, with mitigations designed in rather than bolted on. *Timing closure at the seams* (most likely failure of H3): mitigated by registered seams with scheduler-absorbed skew, the T = 16 fallback, and per-axis demotion to synthesis-time options. *Verification-space explosion*: mitigated by the bounded configuration menu (the enumeration is the spec), formal closure at the MAC, protocol-level model checking for streams, and transition-pair coverage rather than full mode-sequence coverage. *Scheduler complexity creep*: mitigated by the hard rule that runtime only selects, never searches, and by capping persona tables per model. *Precision-composition arithmetic corners* (BitSys's sign/carry hazards): mitigated by formal equivalence as a gate, not a goal. *Sparse-stream misalignment or deadlock*: mitigated by length-announced streams (no in-fabric speculation) and model-checked protocol. *Analytical-model drift*: mitigated by the ±5% calibration gate with regression locking. *The kitchen-sink failure mode* — the honest worst case, in which the union of features is mediocre everywhere: this is precisely what H1's two-sided criterion detects, and the feature-flag architecture means the salvage path (shipping the two or three axes that earn their area, per deployment class) is the same artifact, not a redesign.

## 7. Discussion: Positioning and Outlook

PRISM's relationship to its sources is deliberately conservative where they are strong and aggressive only where they are silent. From Flex-TPU it keeps the stationary-operand mux and the per-layer-selection evidence; from CogSys, the four-register PE, bubble streaming, one-cycle switching, and the heterogeneous-versus-reconfigurable PE verdict; from SNAKE, serpentine remapping, granularity-8 pragmatism, buffer-to-compute reallocation, boundary-side multi-port discipline, and the four-mode multi-core scheduling decomposition (which PRISM's compiler adopts wholesale for multi-fabric scaling); from BitSys, mask-based precision composition and valid-bit discipline; from FlexiSAGA, the bitmap/CSB formats, the DecU placement, and pruning co-designed to hardware vector lengths; from ONE-SA, the CPWL mathematics and the boundary parameter machinery; from NSFlow, the trace-to-graph compiler front end and phased DSE. What PRISM adds — the seam hierarchy, the routing-program unification, resident epilogues, precision-shape exchange, configuration wavefronts, spatial mode co-residence, and the persona runtime — are exactly the pieces that only become visible, or only become valuable, when the axes are considered together.

Beyond the immediate proposal, two outgrowths merit note. First, the feature-flagged generator plus calibrated models constitute an evaluation *instrument* for the field, independent of PRISM's own fortunes: the marginal-utility table of Section 6.6 would, for the first time, let an accelerator team answer "which flexibility is worth its area for my workload?" with measurements rather than seven incommensurable papers. Second, the routing-program abstraction suggests a longer-term research line: if a dozen logical machines fit in a 16-bit tile word today, the natural next questions are compiler-discovered machines (search over routing programs rather than an enumerated menu) and learned cost models that price novel configurations — a controlled step toward CGRA expressiveness that retains systolic timing discipline, taken only as far as the evidence supports.

## 8. Conclusion

Seven recent architectures each prove the same proposition along a different axis: a small steering layer around a rigid systolic core converts workload heterogeneity from a utilization tax into a scheduling opportunity, at single-digit to low-double-digit area cost. Read together, they prove more than any proves alone — that the steering mechanisms overlap, that selection must be per-operator and free, that the memory system carries half the burden, and that co-design with training and compilation multiplies the return. PRISM is the architecture those lessons imply: a rigid-core/flexible-seam fabric whose tiles execute routing programs spanning dataflow, shape, precision, sparsity, and function mode; whose epilogues run in place on resident output tiles; whose precision gains are spent as logical geometry; whose reconfiguration hides inside drain wavefronts; and whose scheduler searches offline and selects online. The methodology proposed here is built to hold that architecture to account — with iso-methodology baselines generated from its own codebase, pre-registered hypotheses, and ablations that price every axis — so that whatever the outcome, the field exits with the one thing it currently lacks: commensurable evidence about what flexibility is worth.
